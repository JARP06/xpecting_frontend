The Trimesters

The First Trimester Even before taking that first pregnancy test, you may experience pregnancy symptoms. Whether its fatigue, nausea, breast tenderness, or intense food cravings, these may be signs you're expecting. Pregnancy symptoms may last for the entire first trimester.

The Second Trimester Many early pregnancy symptoms such as fatigue and nausea may improve during the second trimester and sometimes even disappear. But even if this part of pregnancy seems smooth sailing, it's essential to stay up to date on your pregnancy checkups. Aside from routine monitoring, you'll have at least one ultrasound scan and glucose screening to check for gestational diabetes this trimester.

You're in the home stretch, but your baby—and your uterus—still have plenty of growing to do. You'll see your health care provider more frequently, including the possibility of weekly visits once you get closer to your delivery date.

High-Risk Pregnancy Information

A "high-risk" pregnancy indicates a higher chance of health problems or early delivery for the mother or baby. Factors that can make a pregnancy high-risk include:

  • Age 17 or younger, or 35 or older
  • Underweight or overweight before pregnancy
  • Multiple pregnancies (twins, triplets, etc.)
  • Health issues like high blood pressure, diabetes, depression
  • Past pregnancy problems (e.g., premature labor, genetic issues)
  • Smoking, illegal drugs, and alcohol use

Important tips for a healthy pregnancy:

  • Regular prenatal care
  • Healthy diet and doctor-approved exercise
  • Appropriate weight gain
  • Infection prevention
  • Safe sex practices
  • Stress reduction

Medical Care and Testing During Pregnancy

During your first visit, your health care provider will likely do a pregnancy test, determine how many weeks pregnant you are based on a physical exam and the date of your last period, and predict your delivery date. An ultrasound later will help confirm this date.

If you're healthy and have no risk factors, expect to see your provider:

  • Every 4 weeks until the 28th week
  • Every 2 weeks until 36 weeks
  • Once a week until delivery

Your provider will check your weight, blood pressure, and the baby's growth and development regularly. This includes feeling your abdomen, listening for the fetal heartbeat, and measuring your belly. You will also have prenatal tests and at least one ultrasound.

Health care provider options include:

  • Obstetricians
  • Obstetricians/gynecologists (OB/GYNs)
  • Family practitioners
  • Certified nurse-midwives

Nutrition and Supplements During Pregnancy

Nutrition and Supplements During Pregnancy

During pregnancy, it's important to eat nutritious foods that support your baby's growth and development. You need about 300–400 extra calories a day in the second trimester and a bit more in the third trimester. Ensure your calories come from:

  • Lean meats
  • Fruits
  • Vegetables
  • Whole-grain breads
  • Low-fat dairy products

Essential nutrients include:

Calcium

Pregnant women need 1,000 mg of calcium a day. Good sources include:

  • Low-fat dairy products
  • Calcium-fortified products
  • Dark green vegetables
  • Tofu
  • Dried beans
  • Almonds

Iron

Pregnant women need about 30 mg of iron daily. Iron-rich foods include:

  • Red meat
  • Dark poultry
  • Salmon
  • Eggs
  • Tofu
  • Enriched grains
  • Dried beans and peas
  • Dried fruits
  • Dark leafy green vegetables
  • Blackstrap molasses
  • Iron-fortified cereals

Folate (Folic Acid)

The CDC recommends 400 micrograms of folic acid daily for women of childbearing age, and 600 micrograms for pregnant women. Good sources include:

  • Leafy green vegetables
  • Citrus fruits
  • Beans
  • Breads
  • Cereals
  • Rice
  • Pasta

Hydration

Drinking plenty of water is crucial during pregnancy to support increased blood volume and prevent dehydration and constipation.

For a healthy pregnancy, avoid smoking, alcohol, and drugs, get enough rest, and eat a healthy diet.
