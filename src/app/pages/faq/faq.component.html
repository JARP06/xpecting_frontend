A high-risk pregnancy poses potential health risks to the mother, baby, or both. Factors that can contribute to a high-risk pregnancy include:

  • Pre-existing health conditions such as high blood pressure, diabetes, or autoimmune disorders
  • Age (teenage mothers or women over 35)
  • Multiple pregnancies (twins, triplets, etc.)
  • Complications during previous pregnancies
  • Lifestyle factors like smoking, alcohol use, or drug use
  • Problems with the uterus, cervix, or placenta

It is crucial to work closely with your healthcare provider to manage any health conditions and follow recommended prenatal care to ensure the best possible outcome for both mother and baby.

At the first visit, your healthcare provider will likely do a pregnancy test, estimate the pregnancy weeks, and predict the delivery date based on a physical exam and the last period date. An ultrasound later in the pregnancy will help confirm the delivery date.

If you're healthy with no complicating risk factors, most healthcare providers will want to see you:

  • Every 4 weeks until the 28th week of pregnancy
  • Every 2 weeks until 36 weeks
  • Once a week until delivery

Regular checkups will include monitoring your weight and blood pressure, and checking the growth and development of your baby. Prenatal tests and at least one ultrasound are also common.

When choosing a healthcare provider, consider options such as obstetricians, OB/GYNs, family practitioners, and certified nurse-midwives.

Nutrition and Supplements During Pregnancy

Now that you're eating for two, ensure your calories come from nutritious foods that support your baby's growth and development. Avoid cutting calories or dieting.

  • Pregnant women need about 300-400 extra calories a day in the second trimester and more in the third trimester. If you're very thin, very active, or carrying multiples, you'll need even more. Overweight women may need fewer extra calories.

Healthy eating is crucial during pregnancy, so focus on lean meats, fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and low-fat dairy products.

Calcium

Women 19 and older need 1,000 mg of calcium a day. Your growing baby's calcium demands are high, so you'll need extra calcium to prevent losing it from your bones.

  • Good sources of calcium: low-fat dairy products, calcium-fortified products, dark green vegetables, tofu, dried beans, and almonds.

Iron

Pregnant women need about 30 mg of iron daily for making hemoglobin, the oxygen-carrying component of red blood cells. Without enough iron, the body can't make enough red blood cells, and tissues and organs won't get the oxygen they need.

  • Iron-rich foods: red meat, dark poultry, salmon, eggs, tofu, enriched grains, dried beans and peas, dried fruits, dark leafy green vegetables, blackstrap molasses, and iron-fortified breakfast cereals.

Folate (Folic Acid)

The CDC recommends 400 micrograms of folic acid daily for women of childbearing age and 600 micrograms for pregnant women to decrease the risk of neural tube defects.

Your healthcare provider can prescribe a prenatal vitamin with the right amount of folic acid.

Staying Hydrated

Drink plenty of water to support increased blood volume and prevent dehydration and constipation. Avoid smoking, alcohol, and drugs, get enough rest, and eat a healthy diet.
